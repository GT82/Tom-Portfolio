An Easy 10-point SEO checklist for your next website project

April 11, 2015 by Brian Glassman
in Professionalism, Web Design
vuto5phc7n 9Tweet
19,738 creatives received our email newsletter last week. Click here to subscribe for free.

15 years ago, you could design a great website for your client and not have to worry too much about things like SEO best-practices or the latest Google Search algorithm update. You could simply stuff the your site with keywords and be on the first page of Ask Jeeves.

(Or at least that’s what they tell me; 15 years ago I was sneaking cigarettes in-between classes and practicing for my Driver’s Ed exam).

To put it simply, times have changed.

I’ve been a non-smoker for 4 years and Google’s search algorithm is more intelligent and influential than ever.

Designing successful websites for your clients isn’t just a 1-step process anymore. You’ve got to prepare your website projects for Step 2 — long-term search engine and internet marketing success.

You’ve got to prepare your websites for long-term search engine and internet marketing success.

Click To Tweet

Here’s a simple 10-point checklist you can use for your next website project to nudge your clients toward position #1.

Disclaimer: While the following are widely accepted white-hat SEO best practices, they don’t guarantee success. Never promise your clients “page 1” or “position 1” performance. That’s just bad for business.

1. Meta information

Meta information has been repeatedly exploited by overzealous webmasters and no longer impacts search engine rankings like it used to. Here are some quick do’s and dont’s.

  • Don’t create a meta keywords tag
  • Don’t stuff your title tag/description with keywords
  • Do start your title tag with your main keyword/phrase
  • Do keep your title tag length under 55 characters
  • Do keep your description length under 160 characters

2. Local search rankings

Does your client provide local goods and services to customers within a service area? What better way to get them in front of local customers than to sign them up for a Google My Business Listing? (That question was mostly rhetorical.)

Learn more at Google My Business.

3. Website architecture

Google looks at things like a logical, well-organized website architecture when crawling the links on your site.

  • Have you been able to establish a clear hierarchy of pages on your website?
  • Are they all linked together and well organized?

Also be sure to upload an XML sitemap of those pages to the web root.

4. Image optimization

Your client’s audience doesn’t want to read a wall of text any more than you or your client does.

While it is indeed important to provide high quality, crawlable text content on your site’s pages, it’s also important that readers spend time on your site.

By breaking up the text with high quality images (and adding descriptive alt tags to those images), you can appease both users and search engines. Also, be sure to optimize those images for web viewing and keep those file sizes as small as possible without compromising too much quality.

5. Headings and subheadings

Keeping your content organized with short, descriptive headings and subheadings is a great way to keep users on-site and search engines happy.

Be sure that you only include 1 <h1> tag per page, and that <h1> tag includes a keyword or phrase important to that business or particular page.

6. Google Analytics

You wouldn’t open a retirement account without monitoring your portfolio’s performance over time, would you?

Well you can be sure your clients will want to know how the site’s performing over time.

By equipping your clients with the ability to analyze website data, you’re enabling them to make decisions on how to improve the site, potentially opening yourself up to repeat business.

7. Webmaster Tools

Sorry Google, you’re not the only one I’ll mention here. Bing Webmaster Tools is also important for maximizing search performance on Bing, which commands about 19% of the global search market share.

Be sure to submit your XML Sitemaps to both sites, as well as monitor for important errors for a period of time after launching the site.

8. 301 redirects

If you’re re-designing a client’s website, be careful you’re not creating costly 404 errors to go along with that beautiful new site. It’s usually a good idea to pull down the old XML sitemap to use as a reference point for creating 301 redirects. Webmaster Tools also provides 404 error data you can use to create 301 redirects.

Furthermore, try not to create a “blanket redirect;” that is, redirecting all the old URLs to, for example, the new homepage. Take the time to redirect each old URL to a relevant new URL.

9. Privacy policy and contact page

Be sure that the new site includes a top-level navigation menu item for a contact page, and that contact page contains crawlable company information like the company name, address, and phone number (if you mark the address up with Schema, even better!).

Also be sure to include a Privacy Policy if you’re planning on capturing user information with a web form. Check out this typical privacy policy generator if you’re stuck.

10. Don’t spam your name

Ok, this may not necessarily be an SEO tip, but it’s something that can easily be taken too far. Designers who spam their client’s website with “Designed by [insert company name and hyperlink here]” and litter the source code with meta author information and HTML comments.

It’s their site. Use your client’s new site in your portfolio — but keep your name (and website URL) off of it unless you have explicit permission.
